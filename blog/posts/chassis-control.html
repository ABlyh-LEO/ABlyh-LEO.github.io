Semi-Steering Semi-Omni Chassis Control: From Velocity Decomposition to Torque Distribution

2026-05-06 RoboMaster · Embedded · Control

1. Introduction

During the 2025 season of the RoboMaster competition, we experimented with a hybrid chassis configuration: two fixed omni wheels at the front, and two independently steerable wheels at the rear — a "semi-steering, semi-omni" layout. This design combines the omnidirectional maneuverability of omni wheels with the high-torque advantage of steering wheels, but it introduces significant control challenges: six motors (two steering motors plus four wheel motors) must coordinate to produce the desired net force and torque on the chassis, while the governing equations are underdetermined.

This article systematically lays out the complete control pipeline from velocity decomposition to torque distribution. The core steps are:

  1. Determine the target steering angles via velocity decomposition;
  2. Compute the desired net force and torque on the chassis;
  3. Obtain a preliminary force distribution using the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse;
  4. Estimate the normal-force ratio from the center-of-gravity offset, adjust target ratios with wheel-speed feedback, then use ternary search within the kernel space to find the optimal solution;
  5. Apply power limiting and current capping.

2. Coordinate System and Notation

We define the side with the two omni wheels as the front, corresponding to the $+x$ direction. Orthogonal to this in the chassis plane is the $+y$ direction (to the left); the right-hand rule gives $+z$ upward (the direction in which the gimbal is mounted). The four wheels are located in the four quadrants of the chassis. The half length along $x$ is $a$, the half length along $y$ is $b$, $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$, and $\beta = \arccos(a/c)$.

Key notation:

  • $F_x^c, F_y^c, T^c$ — components of the desired net force on the chassis in the $+x$ and $+y$ directions, and the net torque about the $+z$ axis;
  • $v_x^c, v_y^c, \omega^c$ — velocity of the chassis center in $+x$ and $+y$, and angular velocity about $+z$;
  • $F_1, v_1$ — force and velocity of the omni wheel in the first quadrant;
  • $F_2, \delta_2, v_2$ — force, steering angle, and wheel speed of the steering wheel in the second quadrant;
  • $F_3, \delta_3, v_3$ — force, steering angle, and wheel speed of the steering wheel in the third quadrant;
  • $F_4, v_4$ — force and velocity of the omni wheel in the fourth quadrant.

3. Forward Kinematics: Velocity Decomposition

The purpose of forward kinematics is to compute, from the target chassis-center velocity $(v_x^c, v_y^c, \omega^c)$, the desired speed of each wheel and the desired steering angles. Using rigid-body kinematics, the velocity at each wheel is the sum of the translational velocity of the chassis center and the tangential velocity due to rotation:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \cos\delta_2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \sin\delta_2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cos\delta_3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sin\delta_3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \\ v_4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sin\beta & -\cos\beta & -c \\ 1 & 0 & -b \\ 0 & 1 & -a \\ 1 & 0 & b \\ 0 & 1 & -a \\ \sin\beta & \cos\beta & c \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} v_x^c \\ v_y^c \\ \omega^c \end{pmatrix} $$

Denote the left-hand matrix by $\boldsymbol{A}$ and the right-hand matrix by $\boldsymbol{B}$. Since $\boldsymbol{A}$ has a simple structure, its Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse $\boldsymbol{A}^+$ yields an easy solution:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \\ v_4 \end{pmatrix} = \boldsymbol{A}^+ \boldsymbol{B} \begin{pmatrix} v_x^c \\ v_y^c \\ \omega^c \end{pmatrix} $$

From this we obtain the four desired wheel speeds $v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4$, as well as the desired steering angles $\delta_2$ and $\delta_3$ (recovered from the velocity directions).

Why Fix the Steering Angles First?

A natural question is: why not simultaneously solve for the wheel forces and steering angles from the force-distribution equations? Doing so would require solving 6 unknowns (four force magnitudes + two steering angles) from 3 nonlinear equations — a far harder problem. By first fixing the steering angles via velocity decomposition, the force-distribution problem reduces to an underdetermined linear system. This decoupling of steering and force distribution is the single most important architectural decision in the entire control pipeline.

4. Inverse Kinematics: Kalman-Filter Velocity Observation

The purpose of inverse kinematics is to estimate the true chassis-center velocity $(v_x^c, v_y^c, \omega^c)$ from the four measured wheel speeds.

Why Filtering?

Direct use of the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse (i.e., $\boldsymbol{B}^+ \boldsymbol{A} (v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4)^\top$) is simple and fast, but suffers from two problems: measurement noise makes the result non-smooth — unfriendly for downstream PID control — and slipping wheels produce unreliable data whose weight should be reduced. We therefore use a Kalman filter for state estimation.

Filter Design

State vector:

$$ \boldsymbol{x} = (v_x^c, v_y^c, \omega^c)^\top $$

Measurement vector:

$$ \boldsymbol{z} = (v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4)^\top $$

Since direct measurement of chassis acceleration is unavailable, we assume the chassis-center velocity and angular velocity remain constant between consecutive time steps ($\boldsymbol{u}_k = \boldsymbol{0}$). In the inertial frame, the chassis rotates by $\omega\, dt$ during one time step, so the inertial-frame velocity must be rotated:

$$ \boldsymbol{F}_k = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\omega\, dt) & \sin(\omega\, dt) & 0 \\ -\sin(\omega\, dt) & \cos(\omega\, dt) & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The observation matrix is $\boldsymbol{H}_k = \boldsymbol{A}^+ \boldsymbol{B}$ (reusing the forward-kinematics relationship):

$$ \boldsymbol{H}_k = \begin{pmatrix} \sin\beta & -\cos\beta & c \\ \cos\delta_2 & \sin\delta_2 & -b\cos\delta_2 - a\sin\delta_2 \\ \cos\delta_3 & \sin\delta_3 & b\cos\delta_3 - a\sin\delta_3 \\ \sin\beta & \cos\beta & c \end{pmatrix} $$

The five standard discrete-time Kalman filter equations specialize to our case as follows.

Predict step:

$$ \boldsymbol{x}_k^- = \boldsymbol{F}_k \boldsymbol{x}_{k-1} + \boldsymbol{B}_k \boldsymbol{u}_k = \boldsymbol{F}_k \boldsymbol{x}_{k-1} \quad (\boldsymbol{u}_k = \boldsymbol{0}) $$ $$ \boldsymbol{P}_k^- = \boldsymbol{F}_k \boldsymbol{P}_{k-1} \boldsymbol{F}_k^\mathsf{T} + \boldsymbol{Q} $$

Update step:

$$ \boldsymbol{K}_k = \boldsymbol{P}_k^- \boldsymbol{H}_k^\mathsf{T} (\boldsymbol{H}_k \boldsymbol{P}_k^- \boldsymbol{H}_k^\mathsf{T} + \boldsymbol{R})^{-1} $$ $$ \boldsymbol{x}_k = \boldsymbol{x}_k^- + \boldsymbol{K}_k (\boldsymbol{z}_k - \boldsymbol{H}_k \boldsymbol{x}_k^-) $$ $$ \boldsymbol{P}_k = (\boldsymbol{I} - \boldsymbol{K}_k \boldsymbol{H}_k) \boldsymbol{P}_k^- $$

Here $\boldsymbol{x}_k^-$ and $\boldsymbol{P}_k^-$ are the a priori state estimate and error covariance, $\boldsymbol{K}_k$ is the Kalman gain, and $\boldsymbol{x}_k$ and $\boldsymbol{P}_k$ are the a posteriori estimates. Note that $\boldsymbol{H}_k$ depends on the instantaneous steering angles $\delta_2, \delta_3$ and must be recomputed every control cycle.

The process-noise covariance $\boldsymbol{Q}$ and measurement-noise covariance $\boldsymbol{R}$ are the key tuning parameters. $\boldsymbol{Q}$ encodes how much we trust the constant-velocity assumption — larger values make the filter lean toward measurements. $\boldsymbol{R}$ encodes the expected noise level of the wheel-speed sensors — larger values make the filter lean toward the model prediction. With no external velocity reference, one can start by using the direct Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse solution as a baseline and observe the filter's lag to guide tuning. Chi-squared tests can further be incorporated to assess the credibility of individual measurements and improve estimation accuracy.

5. Preliminary Force Distribution: Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse

Once the steering angles $\delta_2, \delta_3$ are determined by forward kinematics, the remaining four wheel motors must collectively produce the desired net force and torque $(F_x^c, F_y^c, T^c)$.

Force-Balance Equation

From force decomposition and torque balance:

$$ \underbrace{ \begin{pmatrix} \frac{b}{c} & \cos\delta_2 & \cos\delta_3 & \frac{b}{c} \\[4pt] -\frac{a}{c} & \sin\delta_2 & \sin\delta_3 & \frac{a}{c} \\[4pt] -c & -c\sin(\beta + \delta_2) & c\sin(\beta - \delta_3) & c \end{pmatrix} }_{\boldsymbol{C}} \begin{pmatrix} F_1 \\ F_2 \\ F_3 \\ F_4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} F_x^c \\ F_y^c \\ T^c \end{pmatrix} $$

Denote this as $\boldsymbol{C}\boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{y}$.

Properties of the Coefficient Matrix

$\boldsymbol{C}$ is $3 \times 4$, so $\operatorname{rank}(\boldsymbol{C}) \le 3$. Physically the three equations are independent (and this can be proven mathematically), hence $\operatorname{rank}(\boldsymbol{C}) = 3$: $\boldsymbol{C}$ has full row rank. By the rank-nullity theorem, $\dim\operatorname{Ker}(\boldsymbol{C}) = 1$ — the solution space is a line in $\mathbb{R}^4$.

Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse and the Minimum-Norm Solution

Since $\boldsymbol{C}$ has full row rank, its Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse takes the compact right-inverse form:

$$ \boldsymbol{C}^+ = \boldsymbol{C}^\mathsf{H} (\boldsymbol{C} \boldsymbol{C}^\mathsf{H})^{-1} $$

All matrices involved are small (the inversion is only $3\times 3$), easily handled on a microcontroller in real time. The minimum-norm solution $\boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}} = \boldsymbol{C}^+ \boldsymbol{y}$ distributes the effort as evenly as possible among the four motors while satisfying the force and torque requirements, avoiding unnecessary antagonism between motors.

6. Force Distribution Optimization: Center-of-Gravity Offset, Wheel-Speed Feedback, and Ternary Search

The minimum-norm solution $\boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}}$ works well in simple scenarios, but it ignores a critical physical fact: during actual motion, the maximum friction each wheel can provide is not equal. The core idea of our optimization is: first compute the normal-force ratio on each wheel from a physical model — this determines the ratio of maximum friction available — then refine the ratios online using wheel-speed feedback, and finally use ternary search along the kernel-space line to find the force distribution that best matches the adjusted ratios.

6.1 Center-of-Gravity Offset and Normal-Force Estimation

Because of the gimbal, ammunition box, and other equipment, the effective center of gravity is usually not at the geometric center of the chassis, and sits at a height $h$ above the ground. When the chassis accelerates, brakes, or turns, inertial forces produce additional moments that redistribute the normal forces on the four wheels.

Let $F_1', F_2', F_3', F_4'$ denote the ground normal forces on the four wheels (not to be confused with the driving forces $F_i$). From the accelerometer we obtain the chassis acceleration $(a_x, a_y, a_z)$. Force and moment balance gives:

  • Force balance in $z$: $F_1' + F_2' + F_3' + F_4' = m a_z$
  • Moment balance about the $y$-axis: the inertial force $-m a_x$ acting at height $h$ produces a moment $-m a_x h$, which must be countered by asymmetry in the normal forces: $-m a_x h + F_1' c + F_2' c - F_3' c - F_4' c = 0$
  • Moment balance about the $x$-axis: similarly, $m a_y h + F_1' c - F_2' c - F_3' c + F_4' c = 0$

These three equations are insufficient to uniquely determine four unknowns. We therefore introduce the rigid-plane assumption: the chassis is a rigid body, the four wheels contact the same plane, and the sums of normal forces on opposite diagonal pairs are equal:

$$ F_1' + F_3' = F_2' + F_4' $$

This yields the complete linear system:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\ c & c & -c & -c \\ c & -c & -c & c \\ 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} F_1' \\ F_2' \\ F_3' \\ F_4' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} m a_z \\ m a_x h \\ -m a_y h \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

The coefficient matrix is full-rank, giving the closed-form solution:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} F_1' \\ F_2' \\ F_3' \\ F_4' \end{pmatrix} = \frac{m}{4c} \begin{pmatrix} a_z c + a_x h - a_y h \\ a_z c + a_x h + a_y h \\ a_z c - a_x h + a_y h \\ a_z c - a_x h - a_y h \end{pmatrix} $$

This result captures clear physical intuition: during forward acceleration ($a_x > 0$), the rear steering wheels ($F_2', F_3'$) gain normal force while the front omni wheels ($F_1', F_4'$) lose it — exactly why the front wheels tend to slip when climbing a slope. Lateral acceleration ($a_y \neq 0$) creates left-right asymmetry.

6.2 Constructing the Target Force Ratio

We care about the ratio of normal forces, not their absolute magnitudes. Normalize the closed-form solution to obtain a vector $\boldsymbol{p} = (p_1, p_2, p_3, p_4)$ with $p_i \propto F_i'$ and $\sum p_i = 1$. The physical interpretation: under the current chassis motion, this is the ideal ratio of maximum friction available at each wheel.

Let $\boldsymbol{k}$ be a unit vector spanning $\operatorname{Ker}(\boldsymbol{C})$ (obtained via Gaussian elimination, with the first component made positive). The solution space is the line $\{ \boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}} + \lambda \boldsymbol{k} \mid \lambda \in \mathbb{R} \}$. We want to pick a point on this line whose component ratios are as close as possible to $\boldsymbol{p}$. The quality of match is measured by cosine similarity:

$$ \cos\theta(\lambda) = \frac{\boldsymbol{p} \cdot (\boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}} + \lambda \boldsymbol{k})}{\|\boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}} + \lambda \boldsymbol{k}\|} $$

6.3 Wheel-Speed Feedback: Adjusting the Target Ratio Online

The normal-force estimate above yields a static ratio — it does not account for the actual contact state between each wheel and the ground. In practice, we dynamically adjust the target ratio based on observed wheel speeds:

  • For each wheel, compute the ratio of measured speed to desired speed (the latter from forward kinematics);
  • If a wheel spins noticeably faster than desired, it is slipping — the ground cannot provide the friction corresponding to its target force. Attenuate that wheel's component in $\boldsymbol{p}$;
  • When the wheel's speed returns to normal, gradually restore its original component.

In other words, the normal-force estimate provides an initial $\boldsymbol{p}$, and the wheel-speed feedback continuously corrects it online. The adjusted target-ratio vector is denoted $\tilde{\boldsymbol{p}}$.

6.4 Ternary Search for the Optimal Coefficient

The problem now is: over a reasonable range of $\lambda$ (e.g., $[-1000, 1000]$), find the $\lambda$ that maximizes $\cos\theta(\lambda)$. Since the cosine function along the line is unimodal within a reasonable interval (only one maximum exists), ternary search provides an efficient solution:

  1. Start with search interval $[l, r]$ and tolerance $\varepsilon = 0.05$;
  2. Evaluate at the two ternary points $m_1 = (2l + r)/3$ and $m_2 = (l + 2r)/3$;
  3. Compute $\cos\theta(m_1)$ and $\cos\theta(m_2)$. Note: if any component of $\boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}}$ is negative, negate the corresponding component of $\tilde{\boldsymbol{p}}$ as well, so that the target direction is consistent with the force direction;
  4. If $\cos\theta(m_1) < \cos\theta(m_2)$, shrink the interval to $[m_1, r]$; otherwise shrink to $[l, m_2]$;
  5. Iterate until $r - l < \varepsilon$.

The resulting $\lambda^*$ gives the optimized force distribution:

$$ \boldsymbol{x} = \boldsymbol{x}_{\text{pre}} + \lambda^* \boldsymbol{k} $$

6.5 Handling Suspended Wheels

When a wheel completely loses ground contact, its speed will be far higher than the others (the motor spins freely). In this case the elaborate ratio adjustment is unnecessary — simply zero out that wheel's force, and restore it once the wheel speed returns to a reasonable range. This is a safety fallback that complements the optimization framework.

7. Power Control

Power control is the final step in the pipeline. Its goal is to respect the power limit imposed by the referee system while preserving the force-distribution ratios as faithfully as possible.

Motor Power Model

We model the power of an individual motor as:

$$ P = k_1 \omega^2 + k_2 I^2 + k_3 I\omega + k_4 |\omega| + a $$

where $\omega$ is the motor speed (after gear reduction) and $I$ is the current. The term $k_1\omega^2$ captures mechanical losses (proportional to speed squared), $k_2 I^2$ captures copper losses (Joule heating, proportional to current squared), $k_3 I\omega$ is the electromagnetic power (product of current and speed), $k_4|\omega|$ captures speed-linear stray losses, and $a$ is a constant term (idle standby power). The coefficients can be obtained by least-squares fitting to experimentally collected data.

Power Allocation Strategy

Power control follows a two-stage strategy:

  1. Steering motors first: estimate the power consumption of the two steering motors (6020). If it exceeds their allocated limit, compute a power factor and scale each steering motor's current accordingly (by solving a quadratic equation).
  2. Wheel motors second: allocate the remaining available power to the four wheel motors (M3508). If the total estimated power exceeds the available power, multiply all wheel-motor currents by a single factor, preserving the force-distribution ratios.

Why Preserving the Force Ratio Matters

A critical engineering detail: when power is insufficient, scale currents (i.e., torques) by a common factor, not powers. If powers were scaled proportionally, the nonlinear relationship between power and current (due to the $I^2$ term) would distort the torque ratios among the wheels, changing the direction of the net force — which would be disastrous for chassis control. Scaling all currents by the same factor preserves the relative proportions exactly; the net force direction remains correct, only its magnitude is reduced.

Mathematically, finding the common current-scaling factor $\lambda$ reduces to solving a quadratic equation $a \lambda^2 + b \lambda + c = 0$: $a$ aggregates the $k_2 I^2$ terms from all four motors, $b$ aggregates the $k_3 I \omega$ terms, and $c$ aggregates the $k_1 \omega^2 + k_4 |\omega| + a$ terms plus the constant idle powers minus the available power budget. For physical reasons (power increases monotonically with current), this equation always has a unique real root in $[0, 1]$.

Current Limit

Before writing the final values to the motor controllers, check whether any current exceeds the motor's hardware limit (20 A for the M3508). If so, scale all wheel-motor currents by the same factor to stay within limits while preserving the force direction.

8. Summary

This article has systematically laid out the core technical pipeline for a semi-steering, semi-omni chassis:

  • Forward kinematics decouples steering from force distribution, reducing a nonlinear problem to two independent linear ones;
  • Kalman filtering provides smooth and robust velocity observation;
  • Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse gives the minimum-norm baseline for force distribution;
  • Center-of-gravity offset + wheel-speed feedback + ternary search form the complete optimization framework: normal-force estimation provides a physically grounded target ratio, wheel-speed feedback corrects it online, and ternary search finds the optimal solution in the kernel space;
  • Power control preserves force-direction fidelity by scaling currents uniformly under power constraints.

A single design principle runs through the entire pipeline: preserve the force ratio. Whether it is scaling currents rather than powers during power control, or using cosine similarity as the objective function in the ternary search, every decision is made to keep the direction of the net force correct. In competition, this matters far more than squeezing the last bit of performance out of any single wheel.
